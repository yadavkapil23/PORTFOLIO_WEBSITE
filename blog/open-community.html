Leading Onboarding Sessions at OPEN Community

Kapil 2024 4 min read

OPEN Community is a student developer community at UPES where I've been an active contributor. One of the most meaningful things I did there was lead onboarding sessions for new members — teaching Git workflows and Flutter basics to students who were just beginning their developer journey.

Why Onboarding Matters

The gap between "I want to learn to code" and "I can contribute to a real project" is huge. Most students know the theory from classes but have never opened a terminal, never made a commit, never resolved a merge conflict. The onboarding sessions were designed to close that gap in a single afternoon.

The goal wasn't to teach everything. It was to remove the fear of the first contribution.

Session 1: Git Fundamentals

I ran hands-on Git workshops for batches of 30+ students. Instead of slides, I had everyone open a terminal from minute one. We covered the core workflow that every developer uses every day:

  • Initializing a repo and making the first commit
  • Understanding branches — why they exist and when to use them
  • Pull requests and code review etiquette
  • Resolving merge conflicts (the moment everyone dreads)

The merge conflict exercise was always the most memorable. I deliberately created a scenario where two students edited the same line, then we resolved it together on the projector. Seeing the conflict markers and then cleanly resolving them demystified something that causes panic in most beginners.

Session 2: Flutter Introduction

The Flutter workshops were for students interested in mobile development. Flutter's hot reload made it perfect for live demos — you could change a color or padding and see it update instantly, which always got a reaction from the audience.

We started with a single screen app and incrementally added state management, navigation, and API calls. By the end, students had a small but complete app they built themselves during the session.

What Teaching Taught Me

Running these sessions improved my own understanding significantly. When you have to explain something to someone with zero context, you quickly discover the parts you only half-understood yourself. Git rebase, for example — I thought I understood it until I had to explain it clearly. That forced me to go deeper.

I also learned that the best technical teaching is 20% explanation and 80% letting people make mistakes and fix them. The students who typed along and broke things learned far more than those who just watched.

Contributing to Open Source Projects

Beyond the workshops, I contributed to Python and JavaScript projects within the community — fixing bugs, writing documentation, and reviewing pull requests from newer members. Code review is a skill in itself: being specific, being kind, and explaining the why behind every suggestion.

If you're a student who hasn't contributed to an open source project yet, start small. Fix a typo in documentation. Close a small issue. The workflow — fork, branch, PR — is the same regardless of the project size, and getting comfortable with it early is one of the most valuable things you can do.
